Plot Summary

In the early twenty-first century, the Tyrell Corporation, during what was called the Nexus phase, developed robots, called "replicants", that were supposed to aid society, the replicants which looked and acted like humans. When the superhuman generation Nexus 6 replicants, used for dangerous off-Earth endeavors, began a mutiny on an off-Earth colony, replicants became illegal on Earth. Police units, called "blade runners", have the job of destroying - or in their parlance "retiring" - any replicant that makes its way back to or created on Earth, with anyone convicted of aiding or assisting a replicant being sentenced to death. It's now November, 2019 in Los Angeles, California. Rick Deckard, a former blade runner, is called out of retirement when four known replicants, most combat models, have made their way back to Earth, with their leader being Roy Batty. One, Leon Kowalski, tried to infiltrate his way into the Tyrell Corporation as an employee, but has since been able to escape. Beyond following Leon's trail in hopes of finding and retiring them all, Deckard believes part of what will help him is figuring out what the replicants wanted with the Tyrell Corporation in trying to infiltrate it. The answer may lie with Tyrell's fail-safe backup mechanism. Beyond tracking the four, Deckard faces a possible dilemma in encountering a fifth replicant: Rachael, who works as Tyrell's assistant. The issue is that Dr. Elden Tyrell is experimenting with her, to provide her with fake memories so as to be able to better control her. With those memories, Rachael has no idea that she is not human. The problem is not only Rachael's assistance to Deckard, but that he is beginning to develop feelings for her.

Themes & Analysis

Blade Runner explores profound philosophical themes such as:

  • What It Means to Be Human: The replicants show more emotion and depth than many humans.
  • Memory and Identity: Rachael's false memories raise questions about the soul and identity.
  • Technology vs. Nature: The bleak cityscape contrasts with dreams of off-world colonies and nature.
  • Mortality: Roy Batty's final monologue reflects on life, death, and the meaning of experience.

Post-Modernism in Blade Runner

Blade Runner serves as a prime example of postmodernism, as it both reflects the characteristics of postmodernity and incorporates elements of the postmodern condition into its narrative.

The film explores various postmodern strategies such as quotation, recycling, pastiche, hyperreality, and the crisis of identity, both in its form, content, and ideological foundation.

In Blade Runner, there is an overarching postmodern identity crisis that affects all characters and elements of the story. The Los Angeles of 2019 is depicted as a city in constant turmoil, made up of a mix of different styles and trends. It lacks a clear geographical center, a coherent past to draw from, a stable history to anchor itself to, and a unified present that could foster a sense of community.

This fragmented state is why the replicants, including Deckard, are drawn to it. They can relate to and mirror the city's chaotic, fractured nature. Their connection with the city stems from a deep, despairing desire for completeness—each character in the film yearns for a place, a history, and a personal narrative to claim as their own.

"All those moments will be lost in time, like tears in rain."
— Roy Batty's iconic final monologue

A key reference in the film is the now-defunct Pan Am airline, which represents Scott's vision of the future. Ironically, this company went bankrupt and no longer exists. Additionally, the 1980s saw a rise in Japanese wealth, with the Japanese purchasing significant amounts of land in the U.S., particularly in Los Angeles. These references appear in the film, reflecting Ridley Scott's future vision.

While Blade Runner is set in the future, it combines elements from various pasts, blending different historical and cultural influences.

The film also quotes from various film genres, movements, and periods, incorporating scenes directly from older films like Metropolis (a science fiction classic) and Mildred Pierce (a film noir). Additionally, it uses references to pop culture, such as the New York skyline and the pulp fiction of Raymond Chandler.

As Baudrillard notes, Blade Runner is a key example of postmodern cinema, where science, technology, and progress are questioned and shown to have failed. The film's world is plagued by pollution, industry, and overcrowding, with only the wealthy able to escape to off-world colonies. A central theme of the film is the blurring of lines between the real and the artificial, as well as between humans and replicants. As a result, it becomes increasingly difficult to define what it truly means to be 'human.'
